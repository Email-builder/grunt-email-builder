A new public health screening initiative has been developed for hepatitis C virus (HCV)
• HCV infection is a substantial health problem1
• HCV is a leading cause of cirrhosis, hepatocellular cancer, and liver transplantation1,2
• There is a potential for diagnosis at earlier stages of disease1
• Testing for HCV infection is readily available, minimally invasive, and reliable1
Benefits of testing may include facilitation of earlier access to care1
Evidence suggests highest prevalence of HCV among adults born between 1945 and 1965: The Baby Boomers1
Adapted from CDC MMWR Recommendations and Reports 2012 Vol. 61/No.4.
NHANES, National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey.
Updated recommendations expand HCV testing to include patient age—adults born between 1945 and 19651
• All persons diagnosed with HCV should be evaluated for follow-up care as determined by their healthcare provider1
• The CDC states that the targeted birth-year approach could potentially impact HCV-related morbidity and mortality1
• 2012 recommendations by the CDC augment, but do not replace, existing risk-based testing guidelines1*
* Prior to 2012, testing was based solely on risk factors: HIV infection; current or prior history of injection drug use; select medical conditions, including long-term hemodialysis, persistently abnormal alanine aminotransferase levels, or receiving clotting factor concentrates produced before 1987; recipients of transfusions or organ transplants prior to July 1992; needlestick, sharps, or mucosal exposures to HCV-positive blood in healthcare, emergency medical, or public safety work environment; or children born to an HCV-positive woman.
Importance of identifying adults with undiagnosed HCV infection
Reports suggest varying ranges for the rate of fibrosis progression and cirrhosis:
• A modeling analysis of 111 studies including a total of 33,121 patients with chronic HCV found3:
◦ Fibrosis progression is not linear
– The rate of progression was estimated to be higher from stage F0 to F1 than from stage F1 to F2†
– The most rapid rate of progression was found from stage F2 to F3†
† Based on the random effects model, 95% confidence intervals reported for the estimated annual mean stage-specific transition probabilities were: F0→F1 0.117 (0.104-0.130); F1→F2 0.085 (0.075-0.096); F2→F3 0.120 (0.109-0.133); and F3→F4 0.116 (0.104-0.129).
The cumulative probability of cirrhosis increases with number of years postinfection3
